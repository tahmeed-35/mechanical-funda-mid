🔥 Otto Cycle — State Equations
Isentropic T relation
$$\frac{T_2}{T_1} = \left(\frac{V_1}{V_2}\right)^{\gamma-1} = r^{\gamma-1}$$
Isentropic P relation
$$\frac{P_2}{P_1} = \left(\frac{V_1}{V_2}\right)^{\gamma} = r^{\gamma}$$

$$Q_{in} = m\,c_v\,(T_3 - T_2)$$
Work output
$$W_{net} = Q_{in} - Q_{out}$$

$$T_3 = T_2 \cdot r_c, \quad P_3 = P_2$$
After expansion (3→4)
$$T_4 = T_3 \left(\frac{r_c}{r}\right)^{\gamma-1}$$
Isobaric heat input
$$Q_{in} = m\,c_p\,(T_3 - T_2)$$

Engine Cycles

Otto Cycle (SI) · Diesel Cycle (CI) · PV Diagrams

🔥 Otto Cycle (Spark Ignition)

Used in petrol/gasoline engines. Heat addition occurs at constant volume (isochoric).

Thermal Efficiency
$$\eta_{Otto} = 1 - \frac{1}{r^{\gamma - 1}}$$

Key Parameters

  • $r$ = compression ratio $= V_1/V_2$
  • $\gamma$ = ratio of specific heats $= c_p/c_v \approx 1.4$ (air)
  • Typical $r$ for petrol engine: 8 – 12
💡 Higher compression ratio → Higher efficiency, but limited by engine knock (auto-ignition).

🚜 Diesel Cycle (Compression Ignition)

Used in diesel engines. Heat addition at constant pressure (isobaric).

Thermal Efficiency
$$\eta_{Diesel} = 1 - \frac{1}{r^{\gamma-1}} \cdot \frac{r_c^\gamma - 1}{\gamma(r_c - 1)}$$

Key Parameters

  • $r_c$ = cut-off ratio $= V_3/V_2$ (ratio of volume after/before combustion)
  • Typical $r$ for diesel engine: 14 – 22
  • As $r_c \to 1$, Diesel efficiency $\to$ Otto efficiency
💡 Higher $r$, lower $r_c$ → Higher Diesel efficiency. Diesel engines are more efficient than Otto at same compression ratio.

🔥 Otto Cycle — State-by-State T & P Formulas

Step Process Temperature $T$ Pressure $P$ Volume
1 → 2 Isentropic
Compression		 $T_2 = T_1 \cdot r^{\gamma-1}$ $P_2 = P_1 \cdot r^{\gamma}$ $V_2 = V_1/r$
2 → 3 Isochoric
Heat Addition		 $T_3 = \dfrac{P_3}{P_2} \cdot T_2$ $P_3 = \dfrac{T_3}{T_2} \cdot P_2$ $V_3 = V_2$ (const)
3 → 4 Isentropic
Expansion		 $T_4 = T_3 \cdot \dfrac{1}{r^{\gamma-1}}$ $P_4 = P_3 \cdot \dfrac{1}{r^{\gamma}}$ $V_4 = V_1$
4 → 1 Isochoric
Heat Rejection 		$T_1$ (given / initial) $P_1$ (given / initial) $V_1 = V_4$ (const)
📌 For Otto: $\dfrac{T_3}{T_2} = \dfrac{P_3}{P_2}$ (constant volume, ideal gas law). Also note: $T_2/T_1 = T_3/T_4$ (symmetric isentropic processes).

🚜 Diesel Cycle — State-by-State T & P Formulas

Step Process Temperature $T$ Pressure $P$ Volume
1 → 2 Isentropic
Compression		 $T_2 = T_1 \cdot r^{\gamma-1}$ $P_2 = P_1 \cdot r^{\gamma}$ $V_2 = V_1/r$
2 → 3 Isobaric
Heat Addition		 $T_3 = T_2 \cdot r_c$ $P_3 = P_2$ (const) $V_3 = r_c \cdot V_2$
3 → 4 Isentropic
Expansion		 $T_4 = T_3 \left(\dfrac{r_c}{r}\right)^{\gamma-1}$ $P_4 = P_3 \left(\dfrac{V_3}{V_4}\right)^{\gamma}$ $V_4 = V_1$
4 → 1 Isochoric
Heat Rejection 		$T_1$ (given / initial) $P_1$ (given / initial) $V_1 = V_4$ (const)
📌 For Diesel step 2→3 (isobaric): Use $T_3/T_2 = V_3/V_2 = r_c$ (ideal gas at constant pressure). The cut-off ratio $r_c = V_3/V_2 > 1$.

Property Otto Cycle Diesel Cycle
Combustion type Isochoric (const. $V$) Isobaric (const. $P$)
Ignition method Spark plug Compression heat
Typical $r$ 8–12 14–22
Fuel type Petrol/Gasoline Diesel

Fuel–Air Mixture Concepts

AFR · Equivalence Ratio · Lean & Rich Mixtures

⚖️ Air-Fuel Ratio (AFR) & Equivalence Ratio (φ)

Air-Fuel Ratio
$$AFR = \frac{m_{air}}{m_{fuel}}$$

Stoichiometric AFR for petrol: $\approx 14.7$ (meaning 14.7 kg air per 1 kg fuel).

Equivalence Ratio
$$\phi = \frac{AFR_{stoich}}{AFR_{actual}}$$

$\phi = 1$: Stoichiometric. $\phi < 1$: Lean. $\phi > 1$: Rich.

Mixture Summary Table

Mixture AFR φ value Characteristic Effect
🟢 Lean $AFR > 14.7$ $\phi < 1$ Excess air Lower power, better fuel economy, ↑ NOx, ↓ CO
⚪ Stoich. $AFR = 14.7$ $\phi = 1$ Perfect ratio Ideal combustion, catalyst works best
🔴 Rich $AFR < 14.7$ $\phi > 1$ Excess fuel Higher power, worse economy, ↑ CO, ↑ unburnt HC

4 Strokes of IC Engine

The complete operating cycle of a 4-stroke internal combustion engine
🌬️
Intake Stroke
Piston moves down. Intake valve opens. Air–fuel mixture drawn into cylinder.
🗜️
Compression Stroke
Piston moves up. Both valves closed. Mixture compressed. Temperature rises.
💥
Power Stroke
Spark plug fires. Combustion pushes piston down. This is the only stroke that produces work.
💨
Exhaust Stroke
Piston moves up. Exhaust valve opens. Burnt gases expelled from cylinder.
🔑 Mnemonic: "I Can Power Everything" — Intake → Compression → Power → Exhaust

Engine Calculations

Efficiency · Swept Volume · Clearance Volume

📊 Engine Efficiency (η)

Thermal Efficiency
$$\eta_{th} = \frac{W_{net}}{Q_{in}} = 1 - \frac{Q_{out}}{Q_{in}}$$
Otto Efficiency
$$\eta_{Otto} = 1 - r^{-(\ \gamma-1)}$$

Where $r$ = compression ratio and $\gamma \approx 1.4$ for air.

📦 Volume Calculations

Swept Volume
$$V_s = \frac{\pi}{4} D^2 L$$
Total Cylinder Volume
$$V_{total} = V_s + V_c$$
Compression Ratio
$$r = \frac{V_s + V_c}{V_c} = \frac{V_{BDC}}{V_{TDC}}$$

🧠 Swept Volume vs Clearance Volume

Swept Volume ($V_s$)

The volume swept by the piston as it travels from BDC (Bottom Dead Centre) to TDC (Top Dead Centre). Also called displacement volume.

  • $D$ = bore (cylinder diameter)
  • $L$ = stroke length
  • Determines engine displacement (e.g., 1.6L, 2.0L)

Clearance Volume ($V_c$)

The volume remaining in the cylinder when piston is at TDC (fully compressed). This space cannot be swept by the piston.

  • Allows for proper valve clearance
  • Smaller $V_c$ → higher $r$ → higher efficiency
  • Too small $V_c$ → risk of engine knock

Laws of Thermodynamics

Zeroth · First · Second — Foundations of energy science
0

Zeroth Law

If two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, they are in thermal equilibrium with each other.

Implication
$$A \leftrightarrow C, \; B \leftrightarrow C \implies A \leftrightarrow B$$
🌡️ This law is the basis for temperature measurement (thermometers).
1

First Law

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another.

Mathematical Form
$$\Delta U = Q - W$$
  • $\Delta U$ = change in internal energy
  • $Q$ = heat added to system
  • $W$ = work done by system
⚡ Conservation of energy — no perpetual motion machine of 1st kind is possible.
2

Second Law

Heat spontaneously flows from hot to cold. Entropy of an isolated system never decreases.

Entropy
$$\Delta S \geq \frac{Q}{T} \quad \text{(Clausius)}$$
♻️ No heat engine can be 100% efficient — no perpetual motion machine of 2nd kind.

Engine Emissions & Combustion Issues

Major pollutants from IC engines · Causes · Effects

💨 Carbon Monoxide (CO)

Colourless, odourless, toxic gas from incomplete combustion.

Causes:
Rich mixture (φ > 1) Insufficient O₂ Cold engine start Poor atomisation
Reaction: $2C + O_2 \to 2CO$ (instead of $CO_2$)

🌿 Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)

Product of complete combustion. Major greenhouse gas.

Causes:
Complete combustion High fuel consumption Long engine runtime
Reaction: $C + O_2 \to CO_2$ (complete combustion)

☁️ Nitrogen Oxides (NOx)

NO and NO₂ formed at high temperatures. Causes smog and acid rain.

Causes:
High combustion temp (>1800K) Lean mixture High compression ratio Early ignition timing
Reaction: $N_2 + O_2 \xrightarrow{\text{high T}} 2NO$

🌑 Unburnt Hydrocarbons (HC)

Fuel that escapes combustion unreacted. Smells bad, causes smog.

Causes:
Rich mixture Misfires Crevice volumes Low temperature zones Short-circuit (2-stroke)

🌫️ Particulate Matter (PM)

Tiny solid/liquid particles — especially from diesel engines (soot). PM2.5 and PM10 are most harmful.

Causes:
Incomplete combustion Rich diesel mixture High engine load Old/worn engines
Health Effects: Respiratory diseases, cardiovascular problems, carcinogenic at high concentrations.

🔥 Engine Knocking

Engine knock (or detonation) occurs when the air-fuel mixture auto-ignites before the spark plug fires, causing a rapid, uncontrolled pressure rise.

Causes of Knocking

  • High compression ratio beyond fuel's octane rating
  • High intake air temperature
  • Advanced ignition timing
  • Low octane fuel (low anti-knock index)
  • Overheated engine / hot spots

Effects

  • Damage to piston, cylinder head, bearings
  • Characteristic metallic "pinging" sound
  • Reduced engine efficiency
  • Overheating of engine components
⚠️ Prevention: Use higher octane fuel · Retard ignition timing · Lower compression ratio · Engine cooling

Numerical Problems — Quick Reference

Gear Ratio · AFR · Equivalence Ratio — step-by-step approach

🔢 Gear Ratio Calculation

Gear Ratio
$$GR = \frac{N_{driven}}{N_{driver}} = \frac{\omega_{driver}}{\omega_{driven}} = \frac{T_{driven}}{T_{driver}}$$

Example

Driver gear: 20 teeth, Driven gear: 60 teeth

$$GR = \frac{60}{20} = 3 \quad \text{(3:1 reduction)}$$

Output torque = 3× input torque. Output speed = ⅓ input speed.

⚖️ AFR & φ Calculations

AFR from masses
$$AFR = \frac{m_{air}}{m_{fuel}}$$
Equivalence Ratio
$$\phi = \frac{AFR_{stoich}}{AFR_{actual}} = \frac{14.7}{AFR_{actual}}$$

Example

$m_{air} = 29.4\,\text{g}$, $m_{fuel} = 2\,\text{g}$

$$AFR = \frac{29.4}{2} = 14.7, \quad \phi = \frac{14.7}{14.7} = 1.0 \;\text{(Stoichiometric)}$$
